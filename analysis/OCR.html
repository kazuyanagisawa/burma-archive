OCR Analysis

This section evaluates how optical character recognition performed on a handwritten letter written by Karen medic New Gay while recovering from severe injuries after a Burma Air Force firebomb attack. Because the letter is handwritten, emotionally significant, and linguistically irregular, it provided a strong test case for whether AI could reliably recover fragile testimony from conflict.

Artifact Used

The artifact is a handwritten letter addressed to the “FBR family.” Written while the author was physically unable to speak, the letter expresses gratitude toward caregivers, reflects on recovery, and mourns the loss of a friend. It is therefore both a practical OCR challenge and an ethically significant object within the archive.

Why OCR Was Used

OCR was used to test whether machine reading could help recover handwritten testimony for digital archiving. In theory, OCR can accelerate transcription and make fragile texts searchable. In practice, handwritten documents from conflict settings often resist clean extraction because of uneven handwriting, non-standard grammar, image quality issues, and the emotional urgency of the writing situation.

Ground Truth Transcription

To evaluate OCR performance, a corrected manual transcription was established as the reference text. Paired with the original artifact, it serves as a human-verified comparison partner against which model outputs can be judged.

Original Artifact

Comparison Partner: Ground Truth Transcription

Dear, I thank you so much for you remember me in your prayers, encouraging speech and support me, so I thank you all.

I am not your general but you love me like your real sister so I thank God he send me to you. God will be happy for what you did for me.

Just now I can eat, I can walk, and I can talk slow slow.

Finally, God will fill full my dear FBR’s needs and want to do anything be success, is my prayer in Jesus’ name. Amen.

Best Performer

Among the tested systems, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview performed best. It did not produce a perfect transcription, but it recovered substantially more of the letter’s structure, vocabulary, and emotional content than Tesseract. Tesseract’s output was largely unusable for archival purposes without extensive correction.

Interpretation

The OCR results show that model choice matters enormously when working with handwritten humanitarian testimony. A weak OCR result does not merely introduce small errors. It can obscure the emotional logic of the document and erase key elements such as gratitude, bodily recovery, and mourning. In this case, the strongest model made the artifact much more legible, but human correction remained essential.

This matters for the archive because machine readability is not the same as archival recovery. OCR can assist in making testimony searchable and analyzable, but it cannot be treated as a neutral or complete extraction process. The letter’s meaning only becomes fully clear when AI output is checked against the image itself and interpreted through human close reading.

Conclusion

For this archive, OCR is useful as a support tool rather than a replacement for transcription. The handwritten letter demonstrates that AI can help recover fragile texts, but also that archival accuracy and ethical interpretation still depend on human verification.

Once text has been recovered through OCR and manually corrected, it becomes machine-readable data that can support additional computational analysis. In this project, the corrected transcription was later used for sentiment analysis to examine how expressions of gratitude, grief, faith, and recovery appear across the testimony.
